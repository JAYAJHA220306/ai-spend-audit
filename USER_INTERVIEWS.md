# User Interviews

I had conversations with a few people in my network over WhatsApp
between May 24-25. Out of everyone I reached out to, these three
stood out as the most useful, one student who avoids paying for
anything, one who actively price-shops, and a freelancer who already
tracks her AI spend manually. Pretty good spread of perspectives.
---

## Interview 1 — Rashmi J., Student

**Date:** May 24, 2026
**Role:** Undergraduate student

I reached out to Rashmi because she uses AI tools regularly for
college work. She was pretty quick to respond and we ended up
having a longer conversation than I expected.

She uses ChatGPT but only the free version. When I asked if she'd
ever thought about paying for Plus she kind of laughed it off. Her
whole thing was that she just doesn't see the point — the free
version gets her through assignments and research just fine. What
was interesting is it wasn't even really about money. She just
fundamentally doesn't trust that the paid version is worth it.

**Direct quotes:**
- "I don't feel like I should spend money if I can get the same work done for free"
- "The free version does everything I need honestly"
- "I'd only pay if there was something specific I couldn't do otherwise"

**Most surprising thing she said:**
She said she assumes companies just lock random features behind
paywalls to force upgrades, not because the paid version is
genuinely better. I wasn't expecting that level of skepticism.
It's not a money problem, it's a trust problem.

**What it changed about my design:**
This is why I added the "You're spending well" message for users
with zero savings. The tool shouldn't make people feel bad for
being on free plans — sometimes that's the right call and the
audit should say so clearly.

## Interview 2 — Venkata Manaswi, Student

**Date:** May 24, 2026
**Role:** Student, does development projects on the side

Manaswi was the most switched-on of the three when it comes to
actually thinking about AI tools. She doesn't just use whatever
is popular — she actively shops around.

She mainly uses Gemini and she picked it deliberately. When I
asked why, she said she compared a few options and Gemini gave
her the best mix of features for free. She mentioned she searches
for cheaper alternatives before committing to anything, which is
basically the exact behaviour this tool is built for.

**Direct quotes:**
- "I search for less priced AI and mostly use Gemini"
- "I'm mostly impressed by the various features it offers"
- "I don't just go with the popular one, I look for what actually works for the price"

**Most surprising thing she said:**
She treats AI tools the same way she'd treat any SaaS subscription
— always looking for the best feature-to-price ratio. I expected
students to just default to ChatGPT because everyone uses it, but
she had a real switching strategy. That was unexpected.

**What it changed about my design:**
She made me realize the audit can't just say "this is cheaper" —
it has to say "this is cheaper AND does the same thing for your
use case." That's why every recommendation in the breakdown has
a one-sentence reason that justifies the switch on capability
grounds, not just cost.

## Interview 3 — Ritika J., Freelancer

**Date:** May 25, 2026
**Role:** Freelancer

Ritika was honestly the most useful conversation of the three
because she's basically already doing what this tool does —
just manually. As a freelancer, AI tools come out of her own
pocket so she actually pays attention to the bills.

She told me she checks her AI spend every month and tries to
cut it down. She uses multiple tools and said it adds up more
than people expect. When I showed her what I was building she
immediately got it — "oh so it just tells you where you're
wasting money automatically."

**Direct quotes:**
- "I monthly check my expenditure and try to minimize it"
- "It adds up more than you'd think across different tools"
- "I'd definitely use something that just tells me where I'm wasting money"

**Most surprising thing she said:**
The hardest part for her isn't knowing what to cut — it's
figuring out whether the cheaper alternative is actually as good.
She said she sometimes spends more time researching alternatives
than the money she'd save is worth. That reframed the whole
value proposition for me. The tool isn't just a calculator,
it's a research shortcut.

**What it changed about my design:**
The recommendation reasons in the audit breakdown exist because
of this conversation. Ritika wouldn't switch tools based on
"it's cheaper." She needs to know it's cheaper AND equivalent.
So every recommendation now explains the why, not just the what.
